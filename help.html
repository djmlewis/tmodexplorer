Introduction

The purpose of this app is to be a data visualisation tool - nothing more. No statistical analysis is done and you must be aware of the risk of false discovery. However, it leverages the unique design of BIOVACSAFE in that the same chip was used in the same clinical sites, and so if the same genes or modules keep popping-up across different vaccines (or the reverse) this has some significance. Similarly the fact that we have a complete time course allows for some discrimination based on the response curve and adjacent points. While False Discovery statistics can be applied to two-point comparisons (using packages like tmod or limma) where every gene is considered possibly important in the setting of every other gene, this tool allows a more nuanced approach to gene responses to the vaccines tested based on the kinetics and comparison between vaccines as well as placebos.

The purpose is just to visualise the expression of genes as a time course after immunisation, either alone or when clustered into Blood Transcriptional Modules (BTMs). This mimics the usual way immunologists observe cell or antibody kinetics. In addition it allows to look inside the BTMs to observe how the individual genes are responding to the vaccine stimulus.

The datasets are composed of rows of data where each row is a spot on the chip, and the columns contain the group mean value (raw expression or fold-change from baseline) for that spot. The groups are a specific treatment~time point combination: e.g. Boostrix~Day 1, Fluad~Day 7, etc. I use the term "spot" and "row" interchangeably as they are the same. One "Probe" ( specific nucleic acid sequence) may map to many spots, and one gene may map to many probes. Row / ProbeNames are unique, GeneNames may be duplicated across rows.

The simplest approach is to pick a particular vaccine~day combination  - e.g. Day 7 after immunisation with Boostrix - and then sort rows by the values in that column. This imposes the same row (spot) order on other time points and vaccines. Why do this? Well, because with over 40k spots you have to order them somehow. The grouping by treatment (vaccine or placebo) ~ time combination is actually a natural grouping, if a bit counter-intuitive when comparing between vaccines. You can also order rows by value at any time point for one selected vaccine, rather than specifying a specific day. The question becomes: How do the top N genes in the day 1 response to Fluad behave on day 1 after Boostrix? Or maybe: How do the top N genes on day 1 after Fluad behave over time?

Having ordered the top or bottom spots, or found spots with values between a range, you can then group the spots or genes into a set of interest - e.g. the top 10 fold-increased genes on day 7 after Boostrix. This set of selected spots->probes->genes becomes the core building-block of subsequent analysis. You can plot how the expression changes over time (in all the treatment groups); which modules the genes belong to; observe how the other genes in those modules are responding over time; or how the whole module responds over time. You can treat these rows as a 'pseudo-module' and see how this module changes over time in comparison with the predefined modules in tmod. Other search options allow you to select spots/probes/genes by their title or description instead of (or as well as) their expression values, and then follow these selected genes and the modules that contain them over time.

The figures below illustrates this: the spots have been sorted in descending order of their fold-increase from baseline (day 0) in log2 expression value in the participants 7 days after receiving Stamaril. The top 10 spots with the highest fold-increase have been selected and the time series plotted as individual lines for Stamaril, Fluad and Placebo, or as a boxplot for these spots treated as though they belonged to a pseudo-module.

 

We can then identify the modules to which those genes belong, and plot the changes in those modules over time. Note that while the kinetics of the spots is clear-cut, the modules are less so, with some non-responding modules. This reflects that fact that modules contain many other genes that may not be responding to Stamaril at all, or even down-regulated, as  the genes in the modules have been clustered by their authors on the basis of various criteria:
  

When plotting the time-course of genes as an average of the spots, it may become apparent by wide SEM ribbons that spots mapping the same gene are behaving discordantly. It is possible to split the gene averages back into the component spots to identify which genes have aberrant spots using the Gene⫷Spots option. For example, as shown in the figure below for some top genes upregulated on day 7 after Stamaril, while the two IFIT2 spots (red) respond similarly, one of the OAS2 spots (green) is a log2 lower than the other two, and the two RSAD2 spots are far apart (blue). These discrepancies are also seen in the wide SEM ribbons.



Choosing the correct day to sort the spots by is important as the kinetics of responding genes varies significantly. For example sorting on day 1 after Fluad brings up genes with a very sharp kinetics associated with cytokines etc. Sorting on day 7 brings up genes mapping to immunoglobulins with a broader kinetics. Notably whereas Day 1 genes map reasonably well to modules, none of the top 10 day 7 genes map to any tmod modules. Sorting on day 5 brings up a new set of genes together with some of the day 7 genes.






There is also an option to go straight to exploring by modules, in which case you just select tmod modules and observe their response over time. Modules can be selected in the same way as individual spots or genes - by identifying those most up- or down-regulated modules (based on median or mean value of all spots mapping to the module) for a selected treatment ~ time; or by searching for modules by text keyword. The basic concept is the same, as shown here where the top 10 modules up-regulated on day 7 after Stamaril are identified, and the response over time plotted. Modules can be further selected by Title and Category. The plots below actually show the 8 Titles that these top 10 modules belong to. Note that the kinetics is more clear-cut because we have forced the selection to filter on the mean or median expression value or fold-increase of modules. However, as we have selected by module this may not be very representative of the actual top responding genes (as shown above), because the important genes may be clustered with non-responding genes in other modules.
  

By visualising the changes in gene and module expression we can ask how do the tmod modules respond after immunisation and how does this compare when we select individual genes? Are there other gene groupings that may be better? Do some BTMs have so many non-responding genes that they are not representative? Could we cluster new modules?

Plots

Plots may take some time to appear - be patient! The more rows you select for plotting the slower it will be. ggplot2 is not good at providing progress updates - so it may look like nothing is happening. Some plots are drawn automatically and these offer the choice of using R base graphics (faster) or ggplot2 (slower but may be prettier). Other plots have a Plot button, and will only draw or update when that is clicked, to avoid slow refreshes when you alter several options. Plots have a slider control  to change the height. Plot width always resizes automatically with the window width. A low resolution image of plots can be copied or saved by right-clicking on the plot, while clicking ⤓ Plot As PNG button downloads a high resolution image. The plotting parameters are not sophisticated, but generally the data used to create the plot are in a table below and can be saved and imported into R or other packages for plotting. Some tables can be saved as PNG files to incorporate with plots as graphics.

Identifying data on line plots by clicking or selecting points

When data are plotted as Lines it is sometimes difficult to identify the individual data series by colour. They can be identified by clicking a point (or where a point would be plotted if you are not displaying points). Clicking line segments between points does nothing. Click and drag a rectangle to include multiple points. A table will appear displaying the data, which can be copied. A comma-separated list of gene/spot and module names is also shown to easily copy and paste into search boxes.

Data Tables

Where a data table is shown, the entire table can be searched using the box above,  or individual columns searched using the boxes below each column. Tables can be downloaded as a csv file suitable for opening in Excel or R by clicking the ⤓ Table button. A PNG image of the data table can also be downloaded in some cases by clicking the button ⤓ Table as PNG. This can be pasted onto graphics. It is very tricky to calculate the width correctly using grid.table, so the image may be clipped or extra wide, but a transparent background will be generated.

Saving Gene, ProbeNames and Module Lists

The datasets have been normalised together so that ProbeNames, row IDs and Gene names are the same across vaccines. This allows identification of responses for a specific spot/probe/gene between vaccines.
